      How this world works

      You can

      Seed materials into the land — sticks, stones, vines, fire, trees. Where you put things changes what the colony can encounter.

      You cannot

      Control anyone. You can't move agents, teach them, or build for them. The river, the animals, and the generations are beyond your reach.

      What agents do on their own

      They eat, drink, keep warm, run from predators, and raise children. When life is stable, they get curious. Curiosity is guided by their questions — the problems they can feel but can't yet solve.

      The loop

      1. Seed objects into the world.
      2. Watch what problems the colony faces.
      3. Agents ask questions.
      4. Experiments create observations.
      5. Repeated observations strengthen beliefs.
      6. Beliefs unlock applications — tools, structures, techniques.
      7. Applications open new reachable states: futures the colony couldn't reach before, like the far bank of the river.

      Why the river matters

      It splits the world, and it kills. Anyone caught in the water — by a flood, a failing dam, or bad luck — must fight for the bank or drown. Some things only grow on the far side. No one can cross until the colony believes enough about water, wood, and binding to conceive of a bridge — and then builds one.

      Why knowledge can be lost

      Children are born knowing nothing. Beliefs live only in living minds: if everyone who holds a belief dies before teaching it, it fades. A thriving colony is also a school.

      The water is real

      The river is not drawn — it flows downhill over real terrain. Dams raise the ground and lakes pool behind them because they must. When a dam fails, the pooled water tears downstream and can carve the river a new course, permanently. Seeding water literally pours water.

      The land needs tending

      Plants are mortal. Berry bushes wither in minutes, vines die back in droughts, and forests walk slowly. The colony stockpiles wood by the camp, but the garden is yours — keep seeding, or the lean times will do it for you.

      Nothing lasts

      Bridges rot, dams crack, shelters sag, pits cave in, fires burn out, rains flood, droughts starve. Every solution is temporary. A resilient colony repairs, rebuilds, and rediscovers — that's the real game.

      Comfort vs curiosity

      A colony with every need met stops wondering. A colony in crisis has no time to wonder. Discovery lives in the space between — keep them a little uncomfortable, never desperate.

      The possibility frontier

      The number at the top isn't a score of things collected — it counts futures unlocked: can they cross the river, eat without bushes, make fire anywhere, outlive their finders? That's the game.
